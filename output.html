Question: Prove that for all real numbers \( x \), the following identity holds: \[ \sin^2(x) \cos^2(x) = \frac{1 - \cos(4x)}{8} \] Answer:We start with the left-hand side (LHS) of the identity and manipulate it to match the right-hand side (RHS): \[ \sin^2(x) \cos^2(x) = \left(\sin(x) \cos(x)\right)^2 \] Using the double-angle identity for sine, \( \sin(2x) = 2\sin(x)\cos(x) \), we can rewrite \( \sin(x)\cos(x) \) as: \[ \sin(x)\cos(x) = \frac{1}{2}\sin(2x) \] Substituting back into the equation: \[ \left(\sin(x)\cos(x)\right)^2 = \left(\frac{1}{2}\sin(2x)\right)^2 = \frac{1}{4}\sin^2(2x) \] Next, we use the double-angle identity for cosine, \( \cos(4x) = 1 - 2\sin^2(2x) \), to express \( \sin^2(2x) \) in terms of \( \cos(4x) \): \[ \sin^2(2x) = \frac{1 - \cos(4x)}{2} \] Substituting this back into the equation: \[ \frac{1}{4}\sin^2(2x) = \frac{1}{4} \cdot \frac{1 - \cos(4x)}{2} = \frac{1 - \cos(4x)}{8} \] Thus, the identity is proven: \[ \sin^2(x) \cos^2(x) = \frac{1 - \cos(4x)}{8} \]
